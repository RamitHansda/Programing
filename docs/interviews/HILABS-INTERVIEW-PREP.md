# HiLabs Interview Prep — Ramit Hansda

---

## 1. Why did you leave Skydo?

> "Skydo was an incredible journey — I joined as one of the early engineering leaders, built the team from scratch, scaled the payments platform, and led us through ISO 27001 and SOC 2 certifications. By early 2026, I felt I had taken the org as far as I could in that context — the foundational systems were in place, the team was stable, and I found myself in maintenance mode rather than builder mode.
>
> I left earlier this month. It was a deliberate decision after 3.5 years. I wanted to take a short, intentional pause to find the right next step rather than jump at the first opportunity."

**Key framing:**
- You left on March 2nd, 2026 — just 10 days ago. There is no gap.
- Say "I recently left Skydo and I'm actively exploring opportunities" — not "I'm between jobs."
- 3.5 years at a startup is a full chapter. Leaving is completely natural.

---

## 2. Why did you leave without a job in hand?

> "Honestly, I could have stayed while looking — but I've always believed that the quality of your next move depends on the headspace you bring to it. When you're in a role, you're interviewing on weekends, distracted, and you end up saying yes to the first decent offer just to escape. I didn't want to do that.
>
> I have 10+ years of experience, a strong financial runway, and I left on good terms. I was in a position where I could afford to be intentional. So I chose to. I want my next role to be the right one, not just the available one."

**If they push — "But isn't that risky?"**

> "It would be risky if I were junior or had a thin track record. But I've built payments platforms, led 12-person engineering teams, and served as CIO — I'm not worried about finding a role. I'm focused on finding the *right* role. That's a different problem."

**The one-liner if you need to keep it short:**

> "I left because I had the financial stability to be deliberate, and at this stage of my career, deliberate beats fast."

**What the HM is really testing:**

| What they fear | What you show |
|---|---|
| You were pushed out / fired | You left voluntarily, on your own timeline |
| You're desperate and will take anything | You're selective and know your worth |
| There's a red flag they don't know about | You're calm, clear, and self-aware |

> Say it without defensiveness. The moment you over-explain or sound apologetic, the HM's alarm goes off. Say it like it's the most obvious and reasonable thing in the world — because for someone at your level, it genuinely is.

## 3. What are you looking for?

> "I'm looking for three things specifically:
>
> **First, real scale.** At Skydo I got to build from 0→1 and scale to meaningful transaction volumes. I want to join a company where the engineering problems are at the next order of magnitude — more users, more data, more complexity.
>
> **Second, a seat at the table.** I've operated at the intersection of engineering and business — I've been a CIO, I've owned roadmaps end-to-end, I've influenced org design. I want a role where engineering leadership is genuinely a strategic function, not just a delivery function.
>
> **Third, a team I can grow.** I've mentored 8 engineers directly. My best work happens when I can invest in people over years, not quarters. I'm looking for a company that takes engineering culture seriously."

**Then tie it back to HiLabs:**

> "From what I've seen about HiLabs — the growth trajectory, the AI-first product, the scale of health data you're processing — it checks all three. That's why I'm particularly interested in this conversation."

---

## 4. Why do you want to join HiLabs as Engineering Manager?

> "A few things genuinely excited me about HiLabs specifically.
>
> **First, the data problem.** I spent 2 years at Goldman Sachs working on petabyte-scale market data systems, and 3.5 years at Skydo building a payments platform processing 10K+ transactions a day with strict guarantees on accuracy and reconciliation. HiLabs is solving a strikingly similar class of problem — messy, high-volume data that needs to be cleaned, standardized, and trusted — except the stakes are healthcare outcomes, not just financial transactions. That intersection of data engineering and real-world impact is exactly where I want to operate.
>
> **Second, the AI-first approach.** At Skydo, I led our GenAI adoption — defined standards for AI coding assistants, drove tooling across the team. HiLabs is building AI into its core product, processing clinical data 100x faster than humans. That's not AI as a feature, that's AI as the engine. I want to be in an environment where AI is the actual product challenge, not an add-on.
>
> **Third, the growth stage.** 300% growth since 2021, $39M Series B, trusted by 5 of the top 10 U.S. health plans — this is the exact inflection point where engineering leadership matters most. I've done 0→1 at Skydo. HiLabs feels like the next challenge: scaling what's already working, under real growth pressure.
>
> And honestly, moving from fintech to healthcare feels meaningful. Payments infrastructure matters — but fixing broken healthcare data that affects patient outcomes? That's a problem worth the next chapter."

**If they ask — "Why not stay in fintech?"**

> "Fintech taught me the engineering discipline — idempotency, reconciliation, fault tolerance, compliance. All of that transfers directly to healthcare data. But I was looking for a domain where the mission felt bigger than transaction fees. Healthcare data quality directly affects how people are diagnosed and treated. That's a harder problem and a more meaningful one."

**Key signals this answer sends:**

| What HiLabs cares about | What your answer shows |
|---|---|
| Technical depth | Petabyte-scale data, distributed systems, payments reconciliation |
| AI experience | GenAI adoption leadership at Skydo |
| People leadership | Led 12 engineers, hiring, mentoring |
| Growth mindset | Scaled 0→1 at Skydo, ready for the next stage |
| Cultural fit | IISc background matches their top-institution team culture |
